# Bobby Fischer Teaches Chess — Notes

## Book Info
- Authors: Bobby Fischer, Stuart Margulies Ph.D., Donn Mosenfelder

## Introduction: How to Play Chess (pp. 1-14)

### The Board
- 64 squares, 8x8, alternating light/dark
- Board oriented with white square at each player's right corner
- Files (columns) labeled a-h, ranks (rows) 1-8

### Pieces (16 per side)
- King: moves 1 square any direction. Cannot move to attacked squares.
- Queen: moves any number of squares in any direction (horizontal, vertical, diagonal). Cannot jump. Most powerful piece.
- Rook: moves any number of squares horizontally or vertically. Cannot jump.
- Bishop: moves any number of squares diagonally. Confined to one color forever. Cannot jump.
- Knight: L-shape (2+1). The ONLY piece that can jump over others. Always lands on opposite color.
- Pawn: moves forward 1 (or 2 from starting position). Captures diagonally forward 1 square. Promotes on reaching the far rank.

### Special Rules
- Castling: King moves 2 squares toward a Rook, Rook jumps to other side. Conditions: neither has moved, no pieces between, King not in/through/into check.
- En passant: If a pawn advances 2 squares and lands beside an enemy pawn, the enemy can capture it as if it had moved only 1 square. Must be done immediately.
- Pawn promotion: Pawn reaching the 8th rank becomes Queen, Rook, Bishop, or Knight (player's choice, usually Queen).

### Key Concepts from Introduction
- Check: King is under attack. MUST escape immediately.
- Three ways to escape check: (1) Capture the attacker, (2) Block the attack (interpose a piece), (3) Move the King (flee)
- Checkmate: King is in check with NO escape. Game over.
- Stalemate: Player has NO legal moves but is NOT in check. Game is a DRAW.

### Frame 3 (p. 19): To the Experienced Player

### Frame 4 (pp. 20-21): The Object of Chess
- TEACHING: Object is to attack enemy King so it cannot escape = checkmate
- Three ways to escape check: capture, interpose, flee
- Diagram A: Black King in check from White Rook. Three escape options shown.
- Diagram B: CHECKMATE — Queen checks, protected by Rook. All flight squares covered. No interpositions. No captures.
- Diagram C: STALEMATE — Black NOT in check, but no legal moves. Game is drawn.

### Frame 5 (pp. 22-23): Checkmate vs. Escape
- TEACHING: Side-by-side comparison positions
- A1: Rook checks King on back rank. King CAN capture (Rook unprotected).
  - Position: White Rook g8, Black King h8, Black pawns g7/h7. White King g1.
- A2: Same but Rook is PROTECTED. King CANNOT capture = checkmate.
  - Position: White Rook g8, White King h7(?), Black King h8, Black pawns...
- B1: Queen checks corner. King CAN capture (Queen unprotected).
- B2: Queen checks, protected by Bishop diagonally. King CANNOT capture = mate.
- C1: Queen checks. King CAN capture.
- C2: Queen checks, protected by Rook. King CANNOT capture = mate.
- KEY LESSON: Whether the checking piece is PROTECTED determines if it's checkmate or just check.

### Frames 6-11 (pp. 24-29): Can the King Capture?
Series of puzzles — "Can the Black King capture the checking White piece?"

**Frame 6** (p. 24):
- White Queen gives check. Bishop protects Queen diagonally.
- Answer: CANNOT capture. Black is checkmated.
- Position (approximate): Black King g8, White Queen h7, White Bishop e4 (protects h7 via diagonal)

**Frame 7** (p. 25):
- White Queen gives check. Bishop is on wrong diagonal.
- Answer: CAN capture. Bishop not protecting Queen.
- Position (approximate): Black King a8, White Queen a7, White Bishop on f3 area

**Frame 8** (p. 26):
- White Rook checks. Knight nearby but NOT protecting.
- Answer: CAN capture the Rook.
- Position: Black King h8, White Rook g8, White Knight e4, Black Pawn h7

**Frame 9** (p. 27):
- White Rook checks. Knight IS protecting the Rook.
- Answer: CANNOT capture. Rook protected by Knight.
- Position: Black King h8, White Rook g8, White Knight e7 (protects g8), Black Pawn h7

**Frame 10** (p. 28):
- White Rook on a8 checks Black King on a7.
- Answer: CAN capture. Rook unprotected, King adjacent.
- Position: White Rook a8, Black King a7, Black pawns b7/c7

**Frame 11** (p. 29):
- White Rook on c8 checks Black King on a8.
- Answer: CANNOT capture. King only moves 1 square, Rook is 2 squares away.
- Checkmate: King's own pieces (Bishop a7, Pawn b7) block escape.
- KEY LESSON: King can only move ONE square, so a checking piece 2+ squares away can't be captured.

### Frame 12 (pp. 30-31): Flight Squares
- TEACHING: Sometimes King can escape by FLEEING to a "flight" square
- A1: Knight checks from b6, King a8 can flee to b8 (flight square exists)
- A2: Same but King's own Knight on b8 blocks the flight square = "smothered mate"
  - KEY CONCEPT: "Smothered mate" — King hemmed in by own pieces, no escape from Knight check
- B1: Queen checks from a7, King can flee
- B2: Queen checks AND covers flight square = mate
- C1: Rook checks, King can flee
- C2: Rook checks, Bishop covers flight square = mate
- KEY LESSON: Checking piece + supporting piece that covers escape squares = checkmate pattern

### Frames 13-18 (pp. 32-37): Three Options Assessment
Now student must decide among THREE options:
- Can capture attacker
- Can flee
- Can do neither (= checkmate)

**Frame 13** (p. 32):
- Two White Bishops coordinate
- Answer: Can do NEITHER. Mated. "The Bishops team up powerfully here."

**Frame 14** (p. 33):
- Complex position with multiple pieces
- Answer: Can FLEE (one square to the left)

**Frame 15** (p. 34):
- White Rook + Pawn vs Black King
- Answer: Can do NEITHER. "White's Pawn stops Black's King from fleeing."

**Frame 16** (p. 35):
- Knight and Rook checking
- Answer: Can CAPTURE attacker

**Frame 17** (p. 36):
- Pawn gives check with Queen and Rook support
- Answer: Can do NEITHER. Mated by a Pawn!

**Frame 18** (p. 37):
- Rook checks, protected by Queen
- Answer: Can do NEITHER. "Checking Rook is protected by Queen. Black is mated."

### Frame 19 (p. 38): Fischer vs. Keres, 1959
- REAL GAME POSITION from Fischer vs. Paul Keres at 1959 Bled Tournament
- Fischer (Black) mated Keres in this position
- "This mate actually happened!" — shows the power of these patterns in real play
- Keres considered one of the top players since 1938

### Frames 20-21 (pp. 39-40): Identifying Guards
- TEACHING: Which White piece guards each escape square?
- Frame 20: Rook checks. Student must identify which piece (Rook, Pawn, Knight, Pawn, Rook) guards each lettered escape square
- Frame 21: More complex — Queen, Bishop, Knight, King, Pawn each guard a square
  - ALSO: "No White piece is giving check" — it's STALEMATE, not checkmate!
  - KEY LESSON: Stalemate is a draw. Being able to distinguish checkmate from stalemate is critical.

### Frame 22 (p. 41): Find the Mating Move
- "Draw an arrow to show White's mating move"
- First "find the move" puzzle — student must identify which piece to move where
- From Fischer vs. Bent Larsen, 1958 Interzonal Tournament, Portoroz, Yugoslavia
- Real game position!

### Frames 23-24 (pp. 42-43): Find the Flight Square
- "Draw an arrow pointing to the one square to which Black can flee"
- Student must identify the single safe square for the King

### Frame 25 (p. 44): Escape by Capturing
- TEACHING: Black appears checkmated but can escape by capturing the Rook
- "Black has only one place to go, and it just happens that a White Rook is occupying that square. The King saves himself by capturing the Rook — so much the better."
- KEY LESSON: Sometimes the only escape IS to capture the attacking piece's support

### Frame 26 (p. 45): King Can't Take Protected Rook
- White King on e5, Black Queen on b8 giving check, Black Rook on a7
- "Can White save himself by playing King-takes-Rook?"
- Answer: NO (checkmate). The Queen protects the Rook area.

### Frame 27 (pp. 46-47): King as Offensive Piece
- TEACHING: "Remember that one King can help trap the other. In fact, in the later stages of the game when most of the pieces have been traded off, the King can be a very valuable offensive piece — and not one that you have to hide away in the corner."
- Position A: White King + Rook vs Black King. White King hems in Black King while Rook checks = MATE
- Position B: Similar but Black has a flight square = NOT mate
- Answer: Position A only
- KEY LESSON: The King becomes an attacking piece in endgames

### Frame 28 (p. 47): Queen + King Mate
- White King on c5, Queen on d7, Black King d8
- "Is Black checkmated?" Answer: YES
- Queen checks, covers all flight squares, King protects Queen from capture

### Frame 29 (pp. 48-49): Capturing with Other Pieces
- TEACHING: "Sometimes Black can avoid check or mate by capturing with a piece other than his King."
- A1: Queen mates (protected by Rook). A2: Same but Black Bishop can capture the Queen.
- B1: Queen mates (protected by Bishop). B2: Black Knight can capture attacker.
- C1: White Rook mates (Bishop prevents Black Rook from capturing). C2: Without the Bishop blocking, Black's Rook captures White's Rook.
- KEY LESSON: ANY piece can defend — not just the King. Look for defensive resources in ALL your pieces.

### Frames 30-35 (pp. 50-55): Four-Option Defense Puzzles
Now student chooses from FOUR options:
1. King-takes-attacker
2. King flees
3. Other piece takes attacker
4. No defense: checkmate

**Frame 30**: Answer: no defense, checkmate
**Frame 31**: Answer: other piece-takes-Rook (Knight captures)
**Frame 32**: Answer: King-takes-Rook
**Frame 33**: Answer: King flees
**Frame 34**: Answer: King flees
**Frame 35**: Answer: other piece-takes-Knight (Pawn captures the checking Knight)

### Frame 36 (pp. 56-57): Interposition
- TEACHING: "Another way for Black to escape check is to 'interpose' one of his pieces between the checking piece and his King."
- Position A: Rook checks, no interposition possible = MATE
- Position B: Same but Black's Rook can interpose (block the check) = saved
- Position C: Black's Bishop interposes
- Position D: Black's Knight interposes
- KEY LESSON: Interposition is the third way to escape check (after capturing and fleeing). ANY piece can interpose — Rook, Bishop, Knight, even Pawn.
- NOTE: You cannot interpose against a Knight check (Knights jump — nothing blocks their path)

### Frames 37-38 (pp. 58-59): Identify the Defender
- Frame 37: White Rook checks. "Which Black piece can defend?" Answer: the Knight interposes
- Frame 38: White Queen checks. "Which Black piece can defend?" Answer: Pawn captures the Queen

### Frame 39 (p. 60): Comprehensive Review Begins
- "In this and the next few frames you will be shown various positions: in some Black is mated; in others he can defend by capturing the White attacker, moving his King, or interposing."
- This is the START of the main puzzle section — all four concepts combined
- "Is Black mated by White's Bishop?" Answer: NO — Rook interposes

### Frames 40-42 (pp. 61-63): Mixed Puzzles
**Frame 40**: "Is Black mated?" Answer: NO — King flees
**Frame 41**: "Is Black mated?" Answer: YES — "You have to watch those Knights!"
**Frame 42**: "Is the position checkmate or not?" Tricky position with multiple pieces.

### Chapter 1 Teaching Progression Summary
The chapter builds systematically:
1. **Frames 1-5**: What is checkmate? Protected vs unprotected checking pieces.
2. **Frames 6-11**: Can the King CAPTURE the checker? (Protected = no, unprotected = yes)
3. **Frames 12-18**: Can the King FLEE? Flight squares, smothered mates.
4. **Frames 19-22**: Real game positions (Fischer vs Keres, Fischer vs Larsen). Find the mating move.
5. **Frames 20-21**: Identifying which pieces guard which squares. Stalemate vs checkmate.
6. **Frames 25-28**: King as offensive piece. Escape by capturing.
7. **Frames 29-35**: Other pieces can defend! Not just the King. Four-option puzzles.
8. **Frames 36-38**: INTERPOSITION — blocking the check. Third escape method.
9. **Frames 39-42**: All concepts combined. Increasingly complex positions.
10. **Frames 43-48**: Complex positions, real game examples (Fischer vs Gligorich 1961, Fischer vs Letelier 1960)
11. **Frames 49-53**: PINS — a pinned piece can't move because it would expose its King

13. **Frames 57-61**: Combined review — pins + promotion + multiple checking pieces
14. **Frame 63**: DISCOVERED CHECK — moving one piece reveals a check from another piece behind it
15. **Frames 62-79**: Complex mate-in-one puzzles combining ALL concepts. Real game positions (Fischer vs Otteson 1957, Fischer vs Surgies). Chapter ends at Frame 79.

### Frame 63 (p. 85): DISCOVERED CHECK — New Concept
- TEACHING: "When the White Rook moves, the White Bishop will be checking. This is called a 'discovered check.' The Rook has an opportunity to move to a square that cuts off the Black King's flight squares."
- The moving piece doesn't give check itself — it REVEALS a check from the piece behind it
- The moving piece is free to go anywhere, including squares that block escape routes
- KEY LESSON: Discovered checks are devastating because two pieces coordinate — one checks, the other attacks

### Frames 66-79: Final Puzzle Series
- "Can White mate in one move?" — yes/no, and if yes, show the move
- Some are mate, some are NOT — student must recognize both
- Frame 68: White CANNOT mate (any check gets captured)
- Frame 71: "Any White piece that dares to check will be promptly captured" — cannot mate
- Frame 73: "This game took three moves!!" — Black foolishly weakened King's defenses early
- Frame 75: Fischer vs Surgies — real game position
- Frame 78: "White Bishop, which is itself pinned, PINS one of the Black Rooks" — a pinned piece can still pin another piece!
- Frame 79: "Again, the pin wins" — CHAPTER ENDS

### Frame 49 (pp. 70-71): PINS — Major New Concept
- TEACHING: A piece is "pinned" when moving it would expose its own King to capture
- A pinned piece LOSES MOST OF ITS POWERS — it can't capture, interpose, or defend
- Three examples showing pinned vs unpinned:
  - A1: Black Queen can capture attacker. A2: Queen is PINNED by Bishop — can't capture
  - B1: Black Knight can interpose. B2: Knight is PINNED by Queen — can't interpose  
  - C1: White Queen can check and mate. C2: Queen is PINNED by Black Rook — can't check!
- "Whenever a piece is pinned to the King, it loses most of its powers. Always examine pins carefully because they are the seeds of many combinations."

### Frame 50 (p. 72): Triple Pin!
- "A fantastic position! Black's Queen, Bishop and Knight are ALL pinned and thus unable to come to the defense of the Black King."
- Student must identify WHICH White piece prevents each Black piece from defending
- KEY LESSON: Multiple pins can paralyze an entire position

### Frames 54-56 (pp. 76-78): Pawn Promotion
- TEACHING: "When your Pawn advances all the way to the other end of the board, it becomes a Queen, Rook, Bishop or Knight, according to your choice."
- Frame 54: Pawn promotes to QUEEN for mate. "Usually it is best to promote to a Queen."
- Frame 55: Pawn promotes to KNIGHT (not Queen!) for mate! "On rare occasions, promoting to a weaker piece is a better play." Had White made a Queen, Black would have queened his own Pawn — with check!
- KEY LESSON: UNDERPROMOTION — sometimes Knight promotion is the only way to mate. This is a surprisingly common tactical trick.

### Frames 57-61: Combined Review
- Increasingly complex positions combining ALL concepts: capture, flee, interpose, pins, promotion
- Multiple pieces can give check — student must find the ONE that mates
- Frame 59: Another smothered mate — Knight mates because own pieces block the King
